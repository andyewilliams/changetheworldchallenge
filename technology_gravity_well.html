The Technology Gravity Well

Technological advancement automates processes and reshapes markets. When technology automates processes without expanding the market, it operates in a zero-sum environment. In such cases, entities that use the technology most effectively can potentially "win" in the marketplace, but this can drive increasing inequality and centralization. As technology accelerates, it may further advance itself, creating a compounding effect. This dynamic, where technology pushes civilizations towards greater centralization and inequality, is referred to as the "Technology Gravity Well."

The Centralized Technology Gravity Well

In a centralized technology gravity well, technology used in a zero-sum environment concentrates wealth and power. An analogy in nature would be if single-celled organisms discovered external processes—similar to "technologies"—that vastly increased their ability to compete. Without limits on the resources they could store or consume, inequality between organisms would grow, with the most successful individuals accumulating the majority of resources. This would continue until one entity controls the maximum possible resources without shutting down vital group functions.

The Decentralized Technology Gravity Well

Alternatively, technology can expand markets and create opportunities for cooperation, pulling civilizations toward decentralization and equality. This “decentralized technology gravity well” emerges when technology operates in a non-zero sum environment, enabling groups to collectively "win" by increasing overall outcomes. In this scenario, the group as a whole benefits from the effective use of technology, leading to increased prosperity for everyone. A natural analogy would be multicellular organisms, where cooperation between cells enhances their collective ability to solve problems, increasing the overall fitness of the organism.

Why This Matters for Cooperation

The concept of the technology gravity well is crucial for understanding cooperation because it highlights the "mathematics" behind multicellularity and collective intelligence. Just as multicellularity leads to vastly different outcomes compared to single-cellular cooperation, there are similar dynamics in human cooperation. When groups collaborate through strong collective intelligence, they can vastly increase the impact of their collective efforts.

At the 2024 Small Island Developing States (SIDS) conference, held earlier this year, discussions focused on how digital platforms, such as collective intelligence, can impact prosperity and resilience. The advance of technology globally was identified as a factor in centralizing economic power in countries that produce technology-based products, leaving other states competing for smaller shares of the global economy. This dynamic, as hypothesized in the centralized technology gravity well, was seen as contributing to rising inequality.

The Solution: Strong Collective Intelligence (CI)

While the rise of technology seems to correlate with increasing inequality, network science offers hope. It shows that groups of interventions working cooperatively can vastly increase their impact on collective outcomes compared to individual efforts. This is where strong collective intelligence comes in. Just as some problems of cooperation can't be solved by single-cellular organisms and require multicellularity, some human problems can only be solved with strong collective intelligence.

Strong CI leverages network effects—patterns that allow groups to cooperate and exponentially increase their impact on collective outcomes. The solution isn't simply deciding which proposal or idea is "best," but rather implementing strong CI to increase the size of the pie for everyone. This allows the group to discover the most intelligent combination of solutions, benefiting all participants.

Breaking Free from Economic Strangulation

One might ask whether strong CI can help Small Island Developing States break free from the economic pressures of centralization. It can be argued that strong CI technology offers a fundamentally different path for economic development, one that requires building new capacities and mobilizing external funding to take advantage of this opportunity.

Applying the Technology Gravity Well to AI

The idea of the technology gravity well also applies to artificial intelligence. With AI, groups of interventions can cooperate to exponentially increase their impact on outcomes, just as network effects in nature increase collective problem-solving abilities. Strong CI, applied to AI, can change the dynamics from increasing centralization and inequality to fostering decentralization and equality.

To summarize, the technology gravity well—whether centralized or decentralized—offers a powerful framework for understanding how technology influences inequality and cooperation. Strong collective intelligence represents a path toward unlocking greater collective problem-solving capabilities and creating a more equitable future.

Imagining the Future

Currently, society operates much like single-celled organisms, with limited cooperation and collective problem-solving capacity. If technologies and processes are developed that mimic the principles of multicellularity, exponential improvements in solving global challenges may be achievable, including those related to worker well-being and economic inequality. By designing these network effects into every process and technology, the world can shift from increasing centralization to greater equality and cooperation.

The Change the World University Challenge encourages participants to think boldly about these challenges, explore new solutions, and contribute to a future where strong collective intelligence leads to greater prosperity for all.
